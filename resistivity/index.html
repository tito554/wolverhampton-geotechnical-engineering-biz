Wolverhampton sits at roughly 163 metres above sea level on a ridge of Triassic sandstone and pebble beds, which makes ground conditions surprisingly variable across the borough. One site might hit competent Sherwood Sandstone at two metres, while another three streets away encounters glacial till over weathered mudstone. Electrical resistivity testing cuts through that uncertainty. We run Schlumberger and Wenner arrays to build a clear vertical profile of the subsurface, showing where clay lenses pinch out and where the sandstone is competent enough for shallow foundations. The method works well across the Black Country, especially where historic mining and backfilled marl pits complicate the geology. When a test pit is not deep enough to reach the bearing stratum, the VES survey fills the gap without disturbing the ground.

A VES survey in Wolverhampton can distinguish between dry sandstone above the water table and saturated silt layers below it – a difference that changes bearing capacity calculations overnight.

Method and coverage

Our field kit for Wolverhampton jobs is built around an ABEM Terrameter with a multi-electrode switching box, running cable spreads up to 150 metres. We set four electrodes in a straight line – two current electrodes send a DC pulse into the ground, and two potential electrodes measure the voltage drop. The instrument calculates apparent resistivity at each spacing increment. As we widen the electrode separation, the current penetrates deeper, so a single sounding can profile down to 30 metres in the Triassic formations common under the city. Data processing runs through RES2DINV for inversion modelling, producing a 2D cross-section where sand, clay, and saturated zones show distinct resistivity signatures. This direct approach helps engineers spot buried services, old culverts, and dissolution features in the underlying gypsum beds of the Mercia Mudstone.

Regional considerations

The Mercia Mudstone Group underlies much of Wolverhampton and contains gypsum bands that dissolve over time, creating voids and collapse features. These dissolution hollows do not always show up on borehole logs because a borehole is just a single point. A resistivity line walked across the suspect area picks up the low-resistivity signature of water-filled voids or the high-resistivity anomaly where a cavity has drained. We have seen this on industrial estates near the city centre, where old brine pumping accelerated subsidence in the 20th century. Skipping a geophysical survey in these zones is a gamble – a footing placed over an undetected void can crack within months. The resistivity data gives the structural engineer a continuous profile, not isolated spot readings, which is what makes the difference between a safe design and an expensive repair.

Top questions

What does a VES survey cost in the Wolverhampton area?

A standard vertical electrical sounding with a single spread centre typically runs between £430 and £920, depending on the array length and the number of depth levels we need to resolve. A full 2D resistivity line with multiple electrodes costs more because of the extra cable handling and processing time. We send a fixed-price quote after reviewing your site plan and the target investigation depth.

How deep can electrical resistivity testing penetrate in the local geology?

In the Triassic sandstone and Mercia Mudstone formations around Wolverhampton, we routinely reach 30 to 40 metres of investigation depth with a Schlumberger array. The actual depth depends on the maximum electrode spread we can lay out on site and the resistivity contrast between layers. Clay-rich horizons attenuate the signal, so we adjust the array geometry to compensate.

Can resistivity surveys detect old mine workings under my site?

Yes, they are one of the most effective tools for picking up abandoned shafts and collapsed workings in the Black Country's shallow coal and ironstone seams. An air-filled void shows a strong high-resistivity anomaly, while a water-filled cavity appears as a low-resistivity zone. We cross-reference the resistivity data with Coal Authority mining records for Wolverhampton to confirm the interpretation.

How long does a resistivity survey take, and will it disrupt site work?

A single VES sounding takes about 45 minutes per spread centre, and a 2D line of 100 metres usually completes in half a day. The method is non-invasive – we push electrodes into the topsoil or use small auger holes in tarmac, so there is no excavation and no drilling noise. Other trades can carry on working around the cable spread as long as they avoid pulling the connectors.

What deliverables do I get after the survey is complete?

We provide a factual report with the raw apparent resistivity curves, the inverted 2D resistivity section, a site plan showing electrode positions, and an interpretative note that ties the geophysical anomalies to the expected geology. For foundation design projects, we include a table correlating resistivity ranges to likely ground conditions, calibrated against any available borehole data.
